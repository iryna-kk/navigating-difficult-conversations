Scene 1 of 3 · Setup

The Situation

It's Monday morning. You're a new manager at a mid-size software company, three months into your role.

Jordan, one of your direct reports, has missed their monthly sales targets for the second consecutive month. You've called a one-on-one meeting to address the performance issue.

Jordan walks in looking defensive. Before you can say a word, they say: "I already know what this is about, and I think it's unfair."

💡 Research shows that the opening 60 seconds of a feedback conversation determine whether the employee feels judged or supported. Your first response matters most.

How do you respond?

You lean forward slightly, nodding. Jordan pauses, then begins to open up...

Scene 2 of 3 · Discovery

A New Detail Emerges

After you invite Jordan to share their perspective, they reveal something unexpected:

"I've been struggling with the new CRM system. My previous manager said they'd arrange training but it never happened. I've been winging it for months — and honestly I'm embarrassed to ask for help."

This is new information. Jordan's poor performance may be a skills gap, not a motivation problem — a crucial distinction for how you proceed.

📚 The ADDIE model reminds us to always analyse before designing a solution. Jumping to consequences without understanding root cause is one of the most common management errors.

What's your next move?

Jordan leaves the meeting looking visibly lighter. You've agreed on a 30-day support plan.

Closing the Loop

The conversation has gone well. Jordan now has a training plan in place, and feels heard. You have one more decision to make: how do you end this meeting?

This moment defines whether Jordan walks out feeling accountable and supported — or like this was purely a disciplinary exercise.

🎯 Effective feedback ends with mutual agreement on next steps and a clear sense of follow-up. Vague endings erode trust and accountability.

Design Notes & Rationale

Instructional Strategy

This module uses scenario-based learning (SBL) to place learners in realistic situations where choices have consequences. Rather than presenting facts directly, the learner discovers principles through decision-making. This approach is grounded in Merrill's First Principles of Instruction and supports transfer of learning to real workplace situations.

ADDIE Framework

Analyse: Target audience = new managers (0–6 months in role). Performance gap = poor feedback delivery skills.

Design: 3-scene branching scenario + 3-question knowledge check. Bloom's Taxonomy levels: Apply and Analyse.

Develop: Built as single-file HTML/CSS/JS, simulating an Articulate Storyline-style deliverable. SVG characters created without stock imagery.

Implement: Delivered via browser, no LMS required for portfolio review.

Evaluate: Knowledge check provides immediate formative data; score displayed at completion.

AI Tools Used

This project demonstrates AI fluency as a core ID competency. Claude AI was used to: draft initial scenario scripts and dialogue, suggest branching logic, generate feedback copy for each choice, and refine the learning objectives. The designer then edited, structured, and coded the final deliverable — demonstrating the one-person team model of modern instructional design.

Portfolio Value

This module functions as a flagship portfolio piece: it solves a real organisational problem (new manager onboarding), demonstrates end-to-end ID thinking (ADDIE), and shows technical execution (interactive eLearning). The topic was chosen because every corporate organisation has managers, making this highly relevant to any hiring manager across industries.
